### Article

The thermal circuit looks straightforward — steam, sauna, cold plunge, pool, repeat — and it is. But first-timers almost always ask the same slightly anxious question at the door: *how long am I supposed to stay in each bit?* It's a fair question, because there's a real difference between a circuit that leaves you glowing and one that leaves you slightly wrung out, and the difference is mostly timing.

Here's a genuinely simple framework you can follow, and then happily forget.

#### The Short Version

For each heat stage — steam room and sauna — think **10 to 15 minutes**, or simply "until you feel pleasantly, thoroughly warm, but not desperate to leave." Then a **short, sharp cold plunge** — anywhere from 30 seconds to a couple of minutes is plenty — followed by a few minutes of **rest** in the pool or garden lounge to let your body settle. That's one round. Most people do **two or three rounds**, and a full, unhurried circuit lands at roughly **90 minutes**.

That 90-minute figure matters, because it's also the sweet spot to aim for if you're planning to have a massage afterward — but more on that below.

#### Stage by Stage

**1. Lemongrass & herbal steam room (10–15 min).** The gentlest heat, and a lovely place to start. The moist, aromatic warmth opens up your airways and eases your muscles into the session. Breathe slowly; there's no rush.

**2. Dry sauna (10–15 min).** A deeper, more intense heat than the steam room, and the one that really quiets the mind. If it's your first-ever sauna, start on the lower bench (heat rises, so it's cooler down there) and build up. The moment you start thinking "I could leave now" is usually the right moment to leave.

**3. Cold plunge (30 sec–2 min).** The bit everyone dreads and then becomes evangelical about. The contrast with the heat is the whole point — it's invigorating, it sharpens the senses, and it's genuinely part of what makes the circuit *work* rather than just feel nice. You don't need to stay long. In, breathe, out. (If the cold plunge specifically gives you pause, our honest guide on [cold plunge benefits and who should skip it](/blog/cold-plunge-benefits-and-risks) is worth a read.)

**4. Pool & garden lounge (5–10 min, or as long as you like).** Rest, float, breathe, let your heart rate come back down. This isn't dead time — it's where a lot of the benefit actually lands. Then, if you're doing another round, back to the steam.

#### The Golden Rules (There Are Only Three)

- **Hydrate throughout.** Heat plus sweat means you're losing water; keep sipping between stages. This is the single easiest way to feel great rather than woozy afterward.
- **Listen to your body over the clock.** These times are a guide, not a target. Warm and pleasant beats "I stayed the full fifteen minutes out of principle."
- **End on cold, then rest.** Finishing with a cold plunge and a proper rest leaves you feeling clear and settled, rather than overheated.

#### Do You Have to Do It in Order?

The classic sequence — steam, then sauna, then cold, then rest — is the one we recommend, and it's how the space naturally flows. But it's your circuit. Some people love the herbal steam and spend most of their time there; others are all about the sauna. The order is a strong suggestion, not a rule. If you want the full reasoning behind why we built it as a connected sequence at all, our piece on [why we built a full wellness circuit](/blog/why-we-built-a-full-wellness-circuit) explains it, and the [steam-versus-sauna differences](/blog/finnish-sauna-vs-herbal-steam-room) are worth knowing.

#### One Important Caveat

This timing advice assumes you're generally healthy and feeling well. Heat and cold are powerful, and there are conditions — pregnancy, blood pressure issues, feeling unwell — where the sensible approach is gentler and shorter, or checking with a doctor first. We wrote a whole honest guide on exactly that: [thermal circuit safety and who should take it easy](/blog/thermal-circuit-safety-who-should-take-it-easy). Please give it a look if any of that might apply to you.

#### The Bottom Line

Ten to fifteen minutes in each heat, a quick cold plunge, a proper rest, and two or three rounds over about 90 minutes — that's the whole formula. Follow it once, listen to your body, and you'll never need to think about the timing again. And if a massage is on the cards afterward, [combining the two in one visit](/blog/combining-massage-and-thermal-circuit) is our single most-loved way to spend an afternoon.

### Article

The thermal circuit is one of the loveliest things you can do for your body — and, precisely because heat and cold are genuinely powerful, it's also worth knowing when to approach it gently, and when to check with a professional first. This isn't a scary caveat designed to put you off. It's the honest, grown-up version of the advice, so you can enjoy the circuit safely rather than pushing through something your body was quietly warning you about.

**A note before anything else:** everything below is general wellness guidance, not medical advice. We're a spa, not your doctor, and we can't clear you for anything specific to your health. If you have any medical condition and you're unsure, the right move is always a quick word with your own healthcare professional. With that said, here's the practical map.

#### High or Low Blood Pressure

Heat causes your blood vessels to dilate and your heart rate to rise; the cold plunge then does the opposite, sharply. For most healthy people this contrast is exactly the beneficial part — but if you have high or low blood pressure, or a heart condition, it's a genuine consideration. Many people with well-managed blood pressure use saunas without issue, but the key words there are "well-managed" and "many," not "everyone." Please check with your doctor first, and if you're cleared, take it gently: shorter heat sessions, an especially brief and cautious cold plunge (or skipping the plunge entirely and easing into the cooler pool instead), and plenty of rest between stages.

#### Pregnancy

Raising your core body temperature significantly during pregnancy — especially in the first trimester — is generally advised against, which makes hot saunas and steam rooms something to approach with real caution or avoid. This is very much a conversation for you and your doctor or midwife, not something to eyeball. If you're pregnant and visiting, you're of course still welcome to enjoy the garden, the pool at a comfortable temperature, and gentler treatments — and our guide on [prenatal massage safety](/blog/prenatal-massage-safety-phuket) covers the bodywork side of the same question.

#### Feeling Unwell — Fever, Illness, Infection

If you've got a fever, an infection, a contagious bug, or you simply feel rough, the thermal circuit is a "not today." Your body is already working hard, and adding heat stress on top asks too much of it — plus, in shared facilities, it's the kind thing for everyone else too. Rest, hydrate, recover, and come back when you're feeling yourself. The steam room will still be there.

#### After Alcohol

This one's simple and worth saying plainly: don't do the thermal circuit after drinking. Alcohol and heat both dehydrate you and affect your blood pressure and balance, and the combination genuinely raises your risk of feeling faint. Save the circuit for a clear-headed part of the day, and enjoy the drink after — ideally with something to eat.

#### If You Feel Dizzy or Lightheaded

Even if you're perfectly healthy, the heat can occasionally catch you off guard. If at any point you feel dizzy, lightheaded, nauseous, or just "off," don't tough it out: leave the heat, sit or lie down somewhere cool, sip water, and let it pass. Tell a member of staff — that's genuinely what we're here for, and we'd far rather help early than heroically late. It usually settles quickly once you cool down and rehydrate. The most common causes are dehydration or simply staying in the heat a little too long, both of which our [stage-by-stage timing guide](/blog/how-long-each-stage-thermal-circuit) helps you avoid.

#### Age and Children

The thermal circuit and the wider spa have a minimum age of 10 at Ton Mai, and even for older children and teens, the heat stages are worth approaching gently and briefly, with a parent keeping an eye on how they're doing. Young bodies regulate temperature differently, so "less and shorter" is the rule.

#### The Cold Plunge, Specifically

The cold plunge is safe and invigorating for most people, but it's the stage most worth easing into rather than diving into — especially if you have any heart or blood pressure concerns, in which case you may want to skip it and use the cooler pool instead. We wrote a dedicated, honest guide on [cold plunge benefits and who should skip it](/blog/cold-plunge-benefits-and-risks) that's worth reading before your first one.

#### The Bottom Line

For most healthy people, the thermal circuit is safe, wonderful, and exactly as good for you as it feels. The short list of "please check first or take it easy" comes down to blood pressure and heart conditions, pregnancy, feeling unwell, and alcohol — and in every one of those cases, the answer isn't necessarily "never," it's "talk to your doctor, then go gently." When in doubt, ask us or ask them. We would genuinely rather you have a slightly shorter, wonderful session than a dramatic one.

### Article

#### Why the Order Matters

Heat is a fantastic warm-up for bodywork. By the time you've moved through the herbal steam, the sauna and a cold plunge or two, your muscles are warm, loose and far more willing to let go than they were an hour earlier. A therapist working on a body that's already been softened by heat can go deeper, more comfortably, and get more done — which means you get more out of the same massage. It's the difference between stretching cold and stretching warm.

So the sequence is: **thermal circuit → rest → massage.** Aim for roughly **90 minutes** in the circuit (our [stage-by-stage timing guide](/blog/how-long-each-stage-thermal-circuit) breaks that down), finish on a cold plunge and a proper rest so you're not overheated walking into the treatment room, and then let the massage do its work on a beautifully prepped body.

#### "But Isn't Sauna After a Massage Also a Thing?"

It is — and people do ask, because you'll see both orders recommended online. The short answer is that *heat before massage* warms and preps the muscles, while *heat after massage* can feel indulgent but may be a bit much for a body that's just been deeply worked. We land firmly on circuit-first for most people, and we explained the full reasoning in our piece on [whether sauna is safe after a massage](/blog/sauna-before-or-after-massage). If you only take one thing from it: circuit, then massage.
